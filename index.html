01
The Basics
How cricket works, from the ground up

Cricket is a bat-and-ball sport contested between two teams of eleven players each. The fundamental objective is elegantly simple: score more runs than your opponent. But within that simplicity lies extraordinary depth — a game that rewards patience, explosive aggression, cunning strategy, and supreme athleticism in equal measure.

The Playing Field

Cricket is played on a large, roughly oval grass ground. At its heart is the pitch — a rectangular strip 22 yards (20.12m) long and 10 feet wide. At each end stand three wooden stumps topped by two small pieces called bails. Together, stumps and bails form the wicket.

Scoring Runs

Running
1–3 Runs

Batsmen physically run between the wickets. Each completed crossing = 1 run.

Boundary — Ground
4 Runs

Ball reaches or crosses the boundary rope along the ground — automatic 4.

Boundary — Air
6 Runs

Ball clears the boundary rope in the air without bouncing — maximum score per ball.

Getting Out — Dismissals

Most common
Bowled

Ball delivered by the bowler hits the stumps directly, dislodging the bails.

Most common
Caught

A fielder catches the ball before it touches the ground after the batsman hits it.

Controversial
LBW (Leg Before Wicket)

Ball strikes the batsman's body (usually leg) on a trajectory that would have hit the stumps, without first touching the bat.

Action
Run Out

While attempting a run, a batsman's ground is broken by the ball hitting the stumps before they reach the crease.

Keeper dismissal
Stumped

The wicket-keeper breaks the stumps while the batsman is out of their crease and hasn't played a shot hitting the bat.

Rare
Hit Wicket / Obstructing

Batsman accidentally dislodges stumps themselves, or wilfully obstructs a fielder trying to make a catch.

Team Roles

Batsman / Batter

Scores runs. Can be an opener (faces first), middle-order, or tailender.

Bowler

Delivers the ball to take wickets. Types: fast/pace, medium-pace, spin (off-spin, leg-spin).

Wicket-Keeper

The only fielder allowed gloves. Stands behind the stumps; stumps batsmen and takes catches.

All-Rounder

Genuinely skilled at both batting and bowling. Extremely valuable — e.g., Ben Stokes, Kapil Dev.

Captain

Sets field placements, decides bowling changes, manages strategy, leads the team.

12th Man

Substitute fielder; cannot bat, bowl, or keep wicket. Used when a player is injured.

02
History
From Tudor England to a global phenomenon

Cricket's journey from a children's pastime in rural England to the world's second-most-watched sport is one of history's great cultural migrations. It is a game shaped by empire, war, rebellion, and genius.

c. 1550
Origins in the Weald
First references to "cricket" appear in court documents in Guildford, England. Likely a children's game played in the forest clearings of the Weald (Kent, Sussex, Surrey).
1660s–1700s
Adult Game & Gambling
Aristocrats and noblemen take up cricket, driven largely by the gambling culture of Restoration England. Large sums wagered on matches. The sport becomes fashionable.
1744
First Laws of Cricket
The earliest known written code of Laws is drawn up. Established fundamentals: pitch length of 22 yards, crease markings, basic dismissal modes.
1787
MCC Founded
The Marylebone Cricket Club (MCC) is founded at Lord's Cricket Ground in London. The MCC becomes the custodian of the Laws of Cricket — a role it holds to this day.
1844
First International Match
USA vs Canada — considered the first international cricket match in history, though neither team played Test cricket.
1877
First Test Match
Australia defeats England by 45 runs at the Melbourne Cricket Ground — the inaugural Test match. The longest format of the game is born.
1882
The Ashes
England lose at home to Australia for the first time. A mock obituary in The Sporting Times mourns "the death of English cricket." The Ashes rivalry is born — the greatest contest in the sport.
1909
ICC Founded
The Imperial Cricket Conference (now International Cricket Council) is established with England, Australia, and South Africa as founding members.
1932–33
Bodyline Series
England's "Leg Theory" — deliberately bowling at the body — inflames Australia. Don Bradman's average drops dramatically. A diplomatic crisis erupts between the two nations.
1971
First ODI
The first One Day International is played between Australia and England at Melbourne. A new era of limited-overs cricket begins.
1975
First Cricket World Cup
The inaugural Cricket World Cup is held in England. West Indies, under Clive Lloyd, defeat Australia in the final at Lord's.
1983
India's World Cup Miracle
Kapil Dev leads an unfancied India to beat the mighty West Indies in the final. Transformed cricket's commercial fortunes forever, opening up the massive Indian market.
2003
Twenty20 Cricket Launched
The first T20 competition is held in England. The shortest format of the game is an instant commercial and public success.
2008
IPL Founded
The Indian Premier League launches under BCCI commissioner Lalit Modi. The franchise T20 league revolutionizes cricket's economics and global reach, making cricketers among the highest-paid athletes in the world.
2019
Dramatic World Cup Final
England beat New Zealand in the most dramatic World Cup final ever — a super over tie decided by boundary count. Controversy and brilliance in equal measure.
2023
India Hosts ODI World Cup
India reach the final on home soil — only to lose to Australia, one of cricket's great upsets in a World Cup final.

Cricket & The British Empire

Cricket spread across the globe almost entirely through British colonialism. Soldiers, traders, missionaries, and administrators brought the game to the Caribbean, South Asia, Australia, New Zealand, Africa, and the Far East. In many colonized nations, mastering the colonizer's game became an act of cultural assertion and, eventually, resistance. The West Indies beating England, India beating Pakistan, Zimbabwe's early successes — cricket became deeply intertwined with postcolonial identity.

"Cricket is an Indian game accidentally discovered by the English." — Ashis Nandy, Indian sociologist

The Over Structure

An over consists of six consecutive deliveries bowled by the same bowler. Each format uses overs differently. In Test cricket there is no limit — the game ends when 10 wickets fall or the five days expire. In ODIs, each side faces exactly 50 overs. In T20, just 20.

Powerplay Rules (ODI & T20)

In limited-overs cricket, powerplay overs restrict fielding positions — a maximum of two fielders are allowed outside the 30-yard circle. This forces the bowling team to attack, creating exciting early-innings conditions. In ODIs: overs 1–10. In T20s: overs 1–6.

04
Rules & Laws
The 42 Laws of Cricket and modern regulations

Cricket is governed by 42 Laws, maintained by the MCC since 1788. While the fundamentals haven't changed radically, modern cricket has added Playing Conditions that adapt the Laws for international and domestic contexts. The spirit of the Laws is as important as the letter — cricket has an explicit "Spirit of Cricket" preamble.

Key Laws Explained

The Crease

Three lines mark the pitch: the popping crease (batsman's safe zone, 4ft in front of stumps), the bowling crease (where bowler delivers), and the return crease (bowler's foot boundary).

No-Ball

An illegal delivery: bowler oversteps the crease, ball above waist height on full, throws instead of bowls, etc. Batting team gets a free hit (in T20/ODI) and a run; batter cannot be dismissed (except run out).

Wide

Ball passes too far outside off or leg stump for the batsman to reasonably hit. One run added to extras; delivery repeated.

DRS (Decision Review System)

Teams may challenge an umpire's decision using ball-tracking (Hawk-Eye), snickometer (edge detection), and ultra-edge technology. Each team gets limited reviews per innings.

Follow-On (Test)

If Team A (batting second) trails Team B by 200 runs (Test), the fielding side may ask them to bat again immediately. Designed to force a result.

Duckworth-Lewis-Stern

Mathematical method to recalculate targets in rain-affected limited-overs matches. Often controversial but universally used.

Ball Condition & Swing

The cricket ball is a crucial piece of equipment. One side is kept shiny (by the fielding team polishing with sweat and saliva — now replaced by artificial substances after COVID), the other side is allowed to rough up. This differential causes the ball to swing in the air. As the ball ages (beyond ~40 overs in Tests), rough surfaces enable reverse swing — the ball swings unexpectedly in the opposite direction to conventional swing, devastating for batters.

The Spirit of Cricket

"Cricket owes much of its appeal and unique character to the fact that it should be played not only within its Laws but also within the Spirit of the Game." — MCC Laws of Cricket, Preamble

This covers: respecting opponents, not arguing with umpires, not celebrating the dismissal of a batsman by injury, and maintaining the highest standards of sportsmanship. Notable violations include ball tampering (Sandpapergate, 2018, Australia) and underarm bowling (Australia vs New Zealand, 1981).

05
Skills
Batting, bowling, and fielding in depth

Batting

Batting in cricket is a synthesis of technique, footwork, shot selection, and mental fortitude. A batsman must master both attacking and defensive play.

Shot Vocabulary

Cover Drive

Elegant, flowing shot through the off side. The most aesthetically pleasing shot in cricket.

Pull Shot

Horizontal bat swing against a short-pitched delivery rising to chest/shoulder height.

Cut Shot

Played square on the off side against a short, wide delivery. Powerful and clinical.

Hook Shot

Like a pull but played against a ball rising to head height — high risk, high reward.

Sweep

On one knee, bat sweeps the ball to the leg side. Effective against spinners.

Reverse Sweep

Sweep played in the opposite direction — a surprise shot against spinners.

Scoop / Ramp

Delicate deflection over the keeper's head. Modern innovation for T20 cricket.

Slog Sweep

Aggressive version of the sweep, clearing midwicket for six. T20 staple.

Straight Drive

Driven back past the bowler — textbook, classical batting at its finest.

Bowling Types

Fast / Pace Bowling
Speed & Movement

Bowlers delivering at 130–160 km/h. Types of movement:

Outswing — ball curves away from right-handed batsman.
Inswing — curves into the batsman.
Reverse swing — old ball swings unexpectedly.
Seam movement — ball deviated off the pitch seam.
Bouncer — short-pitched, rises sharply.

Spin Bowling
Deception & Turn

Slower bowlers who spin the ball off the pitch:

Off-spin — turns from off to leg (right-arm).
Leg-spin — turns from leg to off; harder to bowl, harder to play.
Googly — leg-spinner's surprise delivery that goes the wrong way.
Doosra — off-spinner's surprise delivery that goes the other way.
Carrom ball — flicked with middle finger, unpredictable direction.

Fielding Excellence

Modern cricket demands elite fielding. Key skills include: ground fielding and diving stops, boundary catches, direct-hit run-outs (throwing at the stumps from distance), and slip catching. Players like Jonty Rhodes (South Africa) and Ravindra Jadeja (India) revolutionized fielding standards. A single brilliant piece of fielding can change a match.

06
Tactics & Strategy
The chess within the game

Cricket is often called "chess on grass." The interplay between captain, bowler, and batsman — field placements, bowling changes, shot selection — forms a fascinating tactical chess match that evolves ball by ball.

Test Match Strategy

In Test cricket, tactics revolve around controlling the game's tempo across five days. Key concepts: building pressure (tight bowling + aggressive fielding = wickets), batting time (survival against quality bowling), declaration (captain declares innings closed to create time for a result), batting order flexibility (night watchman — a lower-order bat sent in to protect specialist batsmen near stumps).

Batting Partnerships

A batting pair is a partnership. Long partnerships demoralize the bowling attack and can change the course of a match. Understanding when to be aggressive vs conservative is the partnership's art. Records like the 624-run unbeaten stand (Mahela Jayawardene & Kumar Sangakkara, 2006) define cricket history.

Death Bowling (T20/ODI)

The last 4–5 overs of a limited-overs innings — the death overs — are where games are won and lost. Batsmen attack furiously; bowlers use yorkers (full, aimed at the batsman's feet), slower balls, and wide off-stump lines to stem the flow. The yorker is the single most valuable delivery in T20 cricket.

Field Placements

A captain sets the fielding positions before every delivery if required. Against a left-hander vs right-hander, for a short ball vs full ball, early in an innings vs at the death — every decision matters. Attacking fields (slips cordon, short leg) invite edges; defensive fields (deep point, fine leg) plug boundaries.

The Toss

Winning the coin toss matters enormously in Test cricket — pitch conditions change dramatically over 5 days. A green, grassy pitch favors batting first; a dry, dusty pitch favors bowling first (as it deteriorates and aids spin). In T20 in dew-affected conditions (especially in South Asia), teams frequently chase because the damp ball is harder to grip for bowlers.

Powerplay Exploitation

In T20, the 6-over powerplay (only 2 fielders outside the ring) is a massive scoring opportunity. Teams target: 150+ run rate in powerplay. Great openers like Rohit Sharma and David Warner built careers exploiting these conditions.

Iconic Series & Rivalries

England vs Australia
The Ashes

The oldest international sporting rivalry. Played every two years, alternating between England and Australia. The "urn" — a tiny trophy containing the ashes of a bail — is the most famous prize in cricket. Ben Stokes' 2019 Headingley innings is the latest of dozens of unforgettable moments.

India vs Pakistan
The Greatest Rivalry

The most-watched bilateral cricket contest in the world. Political tensions make every match electric. These nations rarely play bilateral series, so World Cup encounters between them are sporting events of global magnitude. India leads the head-to-head in World Cup matches significantly.

India vs Australia
Border-Gavaskar Trophy

The preeminent modern Test rivalry. India's back-to-back wins in Australia (2019, 2021) were among cricket's greatest achievements. The 2021 Gabba Test — Australia's fortress breached — is one of the great sporting upsets.

South Asia
Asia Cup

Tournament featuring Asian nations — India, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, Bangladesh, Afghanistan. Played in ODI and T20 formats. A tournament of enormous political and cultural significance in the region.

The Immortals

99.94
Sir Donald Bradman
Australia · Batsman · 1928–1948
The greatest batsman of all time by an almost incomprehensible margin. His Test batting average of 99.94 is the most famous statistic in all of sport. The next greatest averages hover around 60. Scored 29 Test centuries in only 80 innings. During the Bodyline series, England devised an entire bowling strategy specifically to stop one man.

India · Batsman · 1989–2013
The "God of Cricket" and most beloved cricketer in history. 100 international centuries (51 in Tests, 49 in ODIs) — records that may never be broken. First played for India at 16. Carried the hopes of a billion people for 24 years with extraordinary grace. Retired having scored more international runs than anyone.

West Indies · Batsman · 1974–1991
The most destructive and dominant batsman in history — possibly excepting Bradman. The fastest Test century ever (56 balls). Scored a World Cup final hundred. Batted without a helmet, famously chewing gum, exuding power and swagger. The symbol of West Indian pride and postcolonial triumph.

The Sultan of Swing. The greatest left-arm fast bowler in history. Mastered both conventional and reverse swing at pace. His variations — the arm ball, the yorker, the inswing — were virtually unplayable. Led Pakistan to the 1992 World Cup. 414 ODI wickets too.

India · WK-Batsman/Captain · 2004–2020
India's greatest captain and most effective finisher. Won the 2007 T20 World Cup, 2011 ODI World Cup, and 2013 Champions Trophy — the only captain to win all three ICC trophies. Renowned for ice-cold calm under pressure. His helicopter shot and no-look finishes are iconic.

West Indies · All-rounder · 1954–1974
The greatest all-rounder in cricket history. Could bat like a genius (former highest Test score: 365*), bowl fast, bowl swing, bowl wrist-spin, and field brilliantly. First to hit six sixes in an over (1968). The complete cricketer.

Glossary
Every term you need to speak cricket fluently

Master these terms and you'll be able to hold any cricket conversation with confidence.

Ashes, The
The biennial England vs Australia Test series, named for a mock obituary in 1882 for "the death of English cricket."
Bouncer
Short-pitched delivery that rises steeply towards the batsman's head or shoulders. An intimidation weapon.
Century (Ton)
Scoring 100 or more runs in a single innings. The most celebrated batting milestone.
Chinaman
Left-arm wrist-spin bowling. The equivalent of a right-arm leg-spinner's googly. Rare and deadly.
Corridor of Uncertainty
The area just outside off stump where the batsman is unsure whether to play or leave the ball.
Crease
Lines marked on the pitch. The popping crease is the batsman's safe zone. Being behind it = not out.
Declaration
When the batting captain voluntarily ends their innings before all 10 wickets fall to set a target.
Doosra
Urdu for "the other one." An off-spinner's delivery that spins the opposite way, deceiving the batsman.
DRS
Decision Review System — technology (Hawk-Eye, Snickometer) used to review umpiring decisions.
Duck
Getting out for 0 runs. A "golden duck" is out first ball. A "diamond duck" is run out without facing.
Economy Rate
Runs conceded per over by a bowler. Critical stat in limited-overs cricket.
Edge (Nick)
When the ball brushes the edge of the bat, often resulting in a catch behind the wicket.
Extras
Runs not scored by the bat: wides, no-balls, byes (missed by keeper), leg-byes.
Follow-On
When trailing by 200+ runs in a Test, the fielding side can make the opposition bat again immediately.
Full Toss
A delivery that reaches the batsman without bouncing. Considered a poor delivery unless intentional.
Googly
A leg-spinner's surprise delivery that turns the opposite way — from off to leg for a right-hander.
Grubber
A ball that barely bounces — stays very low. Often bowled on deteriorating pitches.
Hat-Trick
Three wickets taken on three consecutive balls (can span two overs or even two innings).
Hawk-Eye
Ball-tracking technology used in DRS to predict the trajectory of the ball for LBW decisions.
Innings
A team's turn to bat. Each team has 1 innings per limited-overs match; 2 in Tests. Plural can be "innings" or "innings."
LBW
Leg Before Wicket — out when the ball hits the body and would have hit the stumps without touching the bat first.
Maiden Over
An over in which no runs are scored off the bat. A sign of excellent bowling control.
Night Watchman
A lower-order batsman sent in near stumps to protect specialist batsmen from the last few balls of the day.
No-Ball
Illegal delivery — bowler oversteps the crease, or bowls above waist height on full. Free hit awarded in T20/ODI.
Over
Six consecutive legal deliveries from one end by one bowler.
Powerplay
Restricted fielding overs in ODI/T20 — maximum two fielders outside the 30-yard circle.
Red Ball / White Ball
Red ball for Tests; white ball (easier to see) for ODIs and T20s under lights. Pink ball used for day-night Tests.
Reverse Swing
Phenomenon where an old, rough ball swings in the opposite direction to conventional swing. Very difficult to play.
Sticky Wicket
A damp pitch drying in the sun — extremely difficult to bat on. Source of the idiom "on a sticky wicket."
Strike Rate
For batters: runs scored per 100 balls faced. For bowlers: balls bowled per wicket taken.
Super Over
Tie-breaking format in T20/ODI — each team faces 1 over to decide the winner.
Swing
Lateral movement of the ball through the air. Inswing comes into the right-hander; outswing goes away.
Tail
The lower-order batsmen (nos. 9, 10, 11) — primarily bowlers who can't bat well. "Wagging tail" = tail batting unexpectedly long.
Test Cricket
The highest form of the game. Up to 5 days, two innings per team. Only elite nations earn Test status.
Ton
Slang for a century (100 runs).
Toss
Coin flip before a match to decide who bats or fields first. Crucial in Test matches.
Umpire
Two on-field officials who rule on dismissals, wides, no-balls. A third umpire reviews technology-assisted decisions.
Wicket
The three stumps and two bails at each end of the pitch. Also used to mean a dismissal ("took 5 wickets").
Wicket-Keeper
The fielder behind the stumps — only player allowed to wear gloves and leg pads.
Yorker
Full delivery aimed at the batsman's feet or base of the stumps. The hardest ball to hit; the bowler's weapon of choice at the death.
