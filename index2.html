Chapter 01
India's Cricket History

Cricket arrived in India through the barrel of empire. British sailors and soldiers brought the game to Calcutta and Bombay in the 18th century — a game for colonizers. Within decades, Indians not only adopted it; they mastered it, weaponized it, and eventually made it their own with a ferocity that no other colonized nation matched. Today, India is world cricket. The BCCI controls roughly 70–80% of global cricket's revenue. Every ICC decision bends toward what India wants. This is the story of how that happened.

1721
First Cricket in India
British sailors play cricket in Cambay (Gujarat). The first recorded game on Indian soil — played by the colonizers, watched with curiosity by locals.
1792
Calcutta Cricket Club
The Calcutta Cricket Club is founded — one of the oldest outside England. Still the British playing among themselves. Indians are spectators, not participants.
1848
The Parsis Lead the Way
The Parsi community of Bombay forms the Oriental Cricket Club — the first Indian-origin cricket club. The Parsis, traders comfortable with British culture, are the first Indians to embrace the game seriously.
1877–1912
The Bombay Quadrangular / Pentangular
Annual tournament in Bombay between teams divided by religion and community — Parsis, Hindus, Muslims, Europeans, and "The Rest." Controversial but enormously popular. India's first mass cricket spectacle. The matches drew passionate crowds — and sharp criticism that cricket was deepening communal divisions.
1911
All India Team Tours England
The first Indian team to tour England — under the Maharaja of Patiala. Not yet official Test status, but India announces itself on the world stage. The tour is modest in results but enormous in symbolism.
1926
India Granted ICC Membership
India joins the Imperial Cricket Conference — the governing body. The path to Test cricket is now open.
June 25, 1932
India's First Test Match
India plays their inaugural Test at Lord's, London, against England. India lose by 158 runs. C.K. Nayudu captains. The Maharaja of Porbandar is in the squad. It is a beginning — and a statement that colonial subjects can play the colonizer's own game at the highest level.
1932–1952
The Struggling Years
India play 24 Tests and win none. The team is talented but inconsistent, undermined by poor infrastructure, political instability (Independence in 1947), and a lack of professional cricketing culture. Vijay Merchant, Lala Amarnath, and Vinoo Mankad shine as individuals — but the team struggles.
1952
First Test Victory
India beat England at Madras — their first-ever Test win. Vinoo Mankad takes 8 wickets in the match. A nation erupts. The wait is over. India can win.
1950s–1970s
The Spin Quartet Era
India find their identity — spin bowling. Bishan Singh Bedi, E.A.S. Prasanna, B.S. Chandrasekhar, and S. Venkataraghavan form the most celebrated spin attack in history. On Indian pitches, they are virtually unplayable. India become genuinely competitive at home, though SENA tours remain brutal.
1971
India Win in England AND West Indies
Under Ajit Wadekar's captaincy, India win series in both England and the Caribbean in the same year. Unthinkable. Dilip Sardesai is the batting hero. The country is transformed. This is the year Indian cricket grows a spine.
June 25, 1983
THE Moment — World Cup Victory
Kapil Dev's India — rank outsiders — defeat the all-conquering West Indies in the World Cup final at Lord's. India defend 183. Kapil's running catch to dismiss Viv Richards is the most iconic moment in Indian cricket history. Everything changes. The Indian market opens. Sponsorships flood in. Television takes notice. Cricket becomes religion.
1983–1990s
The Tendulkar Generation Rises
A 16-year-old Sachin Tendulkar debuts in 1989 against Pakistan. The face of Indian cricket for the next 24 years has arrived. Around him: Azharuddin, Ganguly, Dravid, Laxman, Kumble. The golden generation assembles.
2000–2001
Match-Fixing Scandal & Kolkata Comeback
Indian cricket is rocked by a match-fixing scandal — Hansie Cronje (South Africa) confesses, implicating others. Trust is shattered. Then, the miracle: India forced to follow-on in Kolkata vs Australia, come back to win. VVS Laxman's 281 and Dravid's 180 restore faith. India then win in Chennai too. Series won. Cricket healed.
2007
MS Dhoni & the T20 World Cup
A young Dhoni leads an inexperienced team to win the inaugural T20 World Cup in South Africa, beating Pakistan in a nail-biting final. Joginder Sharma bowls the last over. India win. Dhoni is anointed. A new era begins.
April 2, 2011
Dhoni's Six — World Cup at Home
MS Dhoni hits a six over long-on at the Wankhede Stadium, Mumbai to win the 2011 ODI World Cup. India beat Sri Lanka. Dhoni finishes on 91 not out. Sachin Tendulkar — who has waited 21 years for this — is carried on teammates' shoulders. A billion people weep with joy.
2013
Champions Trophy — The Triple Crown
India win the ICC Champions Trophy, making Dhoni the only captain to hold all three ICC white-ball trophies simultaneously — T20 WC, ODI WC, Champions Trophy. The most successful captain in Indian cricket history.
2018–2021
Conquering Australia
India win consecutive Test series in Australia — 2018–19 (first ever) and 2020–21. The 2021 series is a miracle: depleted by injuries, a young squad wins the Gabba Test — Australia's fortress unbeaten for 32 years. Shubman Gill, Washington Sundar, Shardul Thakur, T. Natarajan — debutants winning where legends failed.
2024
T20 World Cup — The Long Wait Ends
Rohit Sharma's India win the T20 World Cup in the West Indies & USA, beating South Africa in a tense final. Virat Kohli scores 76 in the final. India end an 11-year ICC trophy drought. Rohit and Kohli retire from T20 internationals. A generation signs off on top.
Chapter 02
How Cricket Works

Cricket is played between two teams of 11. One team bats, the other fields and bowls. The objective is to score more runs than the opposition. The game alternates between innings — a team's turn to bat.

The Field

An oval grass ground with a rectangular pitch (22 yards long) at the center. At each end of the pitch stand three stumps topped by two bails — together forming the wicket. Key lines on the pitch: the popping crease (batsman's safe zone), bowling crease (from where the bowler delivers), and return crease (side boundary for the bowler's feet).

Scoring

Running
1–3 Runs

Batsmen run between wickets. Every crossing = 1 run.

Boundary — Ground
4 Runs

Ball reaches or crosses the boundary rope along the ground.

Boundary — Air
6 Runs

Ball clears the boundary rope without bouncing. Maximum per ball.

Getting Out

Bowled

Ball hits the stumps directly, dislodging the bails.

Caught

A fielder catches the ball before it bounces after the batsman hits it.

LBW (Leg Before Wicket)

Ball hits the batsman's body on a trajectory that would have hit the stumps, without touching the bat.

Run Out

Stumps are hit while the batsman is out of the crease during a run.

Stumped

Wicket-keeper breaks the stumps while batsman is out of crease after playing and missing.

Others

Hit wicket (batsman breaks own stumps), obstructing the field, handled the ball, timed out.

Key Roles

Opener

Faces the new ball at the start of an innings. Needs technique and courage against fresh fast bowling.

Middle Order (3–6)

Backbone of the batting lineup. Must be able to build innings and accelerate.

All-Rounder

Contributes with both bat and ball. The most valuable player in limited-overs cricket.

Wicket-Keeper

Wears gloves, stands behind the stumps. Stumps batsmen, takes edges. India has had world-class keepers — Kirmani, More, Dhoni, Pant.

Spinner

India's traditional strength — Kumble, Harbhajan, Ashwin, Jadeja. Spin dominates on subcontinental pitches.

Fast Bowler

Historically India's weakness — now a strength. Bumrah, Shami, Siraj form one of the world's best pace attacks.

The Over

Six consecutive legal deliveries bowled by one bowler from one end = one over. A bowler cannot bowl consecutive overs. In Test cricket, there is no limit on overs — the innings ends when 10 wickets fall. In ODIs: 50 overs. In T20: 20 overs.

DRS — Decision Review System

Technology used to review umpiring decisions. Each team gets 2 reviews per innings in Tests, 1 per innings in limited-overs. Tools: Hawk-Eye (ball-tracking for LBW), UltraEdge/Snickometer (edge detection), Hot Spot (infrared for bat contact). India were the last major team to adopt DRS — a contentious chapter in Indian cricket history.

Chapter 03
The SENA Challenge

SENA — South Africa, England, New Zealand, Australia — are the four nations where conditions are most alien and hostile to subcontinental teams. Green pitches, overcast skies, sideways movement, steep bounce. For decades, winning a Test series in any SENA country was India's Everest. India's transformation into genuine SENA conquerors is one of cricket's great modern stories.

🇿🇦
SOUTH AFRICA
Green, bouncy, pace-friendly

Newlands, Johannesburg, Durban — fast, bouncy pitches. Dale Steyn, Allan Donald, Kagiso Rabada have tormented India here. India have never won a Test series in South Africa. The closest: 2-1 defeat in 2021–22 when India won the first two Tests.

🏴󠁧󠁢󠁥󠁮󠁧󠁿
ENGLAND
Swing, seam, overcast skies

Swing bowling capital of the world. James Anderson, Stuart Broad have made India suffer for decades. India won in 1971 (Ajit Wadekar) and 2007. Recent tours have been mixed — India led 2-1 in 2021 before a COVID-affected final Test. 2024 series: India lose 1–3 under new era.

🟡 Occasional wins
🇳🇿
NEW ZEALAND
Seam, swing, lateral movement

India have never won a Test series in New Zealand. The swinging conditions and pace of Trent Bridge and Wellington have consistently exposed India's batting. NZ upset India in the WTC Final (2021) at Southampton — conditions mirroring New Zealand's home environment.

🔴 Never won a series
🇦🇺
AUSTRALIA
Pace, bounce, intimidation

India's greatest SENA triumph. After decades of failure — including the famous 0–3 loss in 2011–12 — India won consecutive series: 2018–19 (first ever win in Australia) and the historic 2020–21 comeback from the Gabba to claim it again. Pujara's accumulation, Bumrah's hostility, and the young guns — Gill, Pant, Siraj — made it unforgettable.

🟢 Back-to-back series wins

Why SENA is So Hard for India

Pitch Conditions

Indian pitches are flat, dry, slow — perfect for spin and accumulation. SENA pitches are green with moisture, offering pace, bounce, and sideways movement. Indian batsmen trained on home pitches must completely reprogram their technique.

The Corridor of Uncertainty

SENA fast bowlers target the off-stump corridor relentlessly — half-pitch deliveries that force batsmen to play or leave. Indian batsmen raised on spin-heavy tracks historically struggled with this discipline.

Overhead Conditions

Overcast skies in England and NZ assist swing. The Dukes ball (used in England and NZ) swings more than the Kookaburra used in Australia. India's biggest nemesis: James Anderson with a swinging Dukes ball in gloomy Birmingham.

The Mental Game

SENA teams play aggressively — verbal intimidation, short-pitched bowling, fielding in your face. Teams that don't back themselves psychologically crumble. The 2021 Gabba win was as much a mental triumph as a technical one — India refusing to accept defeat.

India's Greatest SENA Moments

1971 — England
Wadekar's series win at the home of cricket
India win a Test series in England for the first time. Dilip Sardesai top-scores. The spinners run through England. India win 1–0. Unimaginable at the time.
2007 — England
Kumble & Dravid's series win
India win in England for the second time under Rahul Dravid. Anil Kumble leads from the front. Zaheer Khan takes 18 wickets in the series.
2018–19 — Australia
History made — first ever series win in Australia
India win 2–1 under Virat Kohli. Cheteshwar Pujara plays 521 balls in one innings. Bumrah announces himself as a global superstar. Australia crumble at home. The impossible is done.

The Greatest Test Series India has ever played
India lose Kohli after the first Test (paternity leave). Bumrah breaks down. Shami breaks down. India field a team of debutants. Yet — they draw Adelaide (after the horror of being bowled out for 36), win Melbourne, draw Sydney, and then — the Gabba. T. Natarajan, Washington Sundar, Shardul Thakur, Siraj, Gill, Pant. India win 2–1. The Fortress falls.
"This Test win at the Gabba is right up there with the greatest achievements in Indian cricket history — perhaps second only to 1983." — Sunil Gavaskar, after the 2021 Gabba Test

Chapter 04
India's Legends

The Gods

100
Sachin Ramesh Tendulkar
Batsman · 1989–2013 · 200 Tests · 463 ODIs
The God of Cricket. 100 international centuries — 51 in Tests, 49 in ODIs. No human has come close. Debuted at 16 against Pakistan in 1989, nicking Waqar Younis to the wicket-keeper — and still held his ground. Carried the expectations of a billion people for 24 years with unimaginable grace, consistency, and humility. His straight drive, his cover drive off the back foot, his pull — textbook perfection. When Tendulkar walked in, India stopped. Offices emptied, streets fell silent. His retirement in 2013 felt like the end of an era for a generation.
619
Anil Kumble
Leg-spin · 1990–2008 · 132 Tests · 271 ODIs
619 Test wickets — India's greatest ever bowler, third highest all-time. Known as "Jumbo." Unlike Shane Warne who spun the ball sharply, Kumble relied on pace, accuracy, and subtle variation. One of cricket's great overachievers — extracting wickets on pitches that offered little. His defining moment: bowling West Indies out almost single-handedly in Antigua, 1999, with a broken jaw strapped in bandages. Led India from the front. Ruthless, proud, excellent.
91*
Mahendra Singh Dhoni (MSD / Captain Cool)
WK-Batsman / Captain · 2004–2020 · 90 Tests · 350 ODIs · 98 T20Is
India's greatest captain and greatest finisher. Won the 2007 T20 WC, 2011 ODI WC, 2013 Champions Trophy — the only captain to hold all three ICC white-ball trophies. His helicopter shot. His lightning-quick stumpings. His ice-cold decision-making. The six that won the 2011 World Cup was not a six — it was a full stop on 28 years of waiting. Retired silently on August 15, 2020 (Independence Day) via an Instagram post. No farewell match. Pure Dhoni.
70
Rahul Dravid (The Wall)
Batsman · 1996–2012 · 164 Tests · 344 ODIs
13,288 Test runs. The batsman who held India together while others attacked. His technique was flawless — compact, upright, absolutely correct. Dravid batted for 44,000 balls in Test cricket — the most ever. His 210 in partnership with Laxman's 281 at Kolkata (2001) is the greatest rearguard in Indian cricket history. India head coach 2021–2024, guiding the team to the 2024 T20 World Cup. Earned the nickname "The Wall" — you simply could not get through him.
281
VVS Laxman (Very Very Special)
Batsman · 1996–2012 · 134 Tests
8,781 Test runs but not defined by numbers — defined by moments. His 281 at Eden Gardens, Kolkata in 2001 vs Australia (forced to follow-on, India came back to win) is the most romantic innings in Indian cricket history. Averaged 45 in Tests but seemed to bat his absolute best when India were in crisis — which is the highest compliment you can pay a batsman.
10,000
Sourav Ganguly (Dada / Prince of Kolkata)
Batsman / Captain · 1992–2008 · 113 Tests · 311 ODIs
7,212 Test runs and arguably India's most transformational captain. Inherited a team reeling from the match-fixing scandal, found a generation of talent (Dravid, Laxman, Sehwag, Zaheer, Harbhajan, a young Yuvraj and Dhoni) and forged them into a unit that was no longer afraid. Ganguly gave Indian cricket its spine. Famously removed his shirt on the Lord's balcony in 2002 after India won a NatWest Trophy final. Passion, aggression, pride — everything Dada played with.
83
Kapil Dev (Haryana Hurricane)
All-rounder · 1978–1994 · 131 Tests · 225 ODIs
India's first genuine fast-bowling all-rounder. 434 Test wickets at a time when Indian bowling meant spin. 5,248 Test runs. But his legacy was sealed on June 25, 1983 at Lord's — captaining India to their first World Cup. His innings of 175* vs Zimbabwe in the 1983 WC (no TV coverage, no DRS — his incredible hitting went unrecorded on video) and the catch to dismiss Viv Richards in the final are the defining images of Indian cricket's transformation.

The Modern Stars

The King
VIRAT KOHLI

80 international centuries (50 Tests, 50 ODIs). The greatest run-chaser in history. Transformed Indian fitness culture. Captained India across all formats, leading the country's rise as a genuine SENA force. His 254* vs South Africa was his masterpiece. Retired from T20Is after winning 2024 WC.

Hitman
ROHIT SHARMA

Three ODI double centuries — the only batsman to achieve this. Revolutionized opening in T20 cricket. Most sixes in T20 internationals. Led India to the 2024 T20 WC. His transformation from ODI middle-order floater to T20 & ODI opener is one of cricket's great tactical stories.

Jassi
JASPRIT BUMRAH

The greatest Indian fast bowler of the modern era. Unorthodox slingy action, deceptive pace, the most lethal yorker in the game. Effective in all conditions — including SENA. World's #1 Test bowler for extended periods. His 2021 Gabba burst with a broken thumb in the previous Test was legendary.

Sir Jadeja
RAVINDRA JADEJA

One of the greatest all-rounders India has produced. Left-arm spin that turns sharply, batting that has won Test matches from hopeless positions, and fielding that is simply the best on the planet. His run-outs are works of art.

Ash
R. ASHWIN

500+ Test wickets. The most intelligent bowler in the modern game. Off-spin with carrom balls, doosras, and variations that baffle the finest batsmen. Consistently ranked the #1 Test bowler in the world. Retired from international cricket in 2024 after the Australia series.

Baby AB
RISHABH PANT

India's most entertaining modern cricketer. The left-handed wicket-keeper who bats like he has nothing to lose. His 89* at the Gabba in 2021 — chasing under pressure, timing impossibly — is already an immortal innings. Survived a near-fatal car crash in 2022, returned stronger.

ICC Champions Trophy

India won the 2002 Champions Trophy (shared with Sri Lanka due to rain) and the 2013 Champions Trophy in England — completing Dhoni's remarkable treble of ICC white-ball trophies. India also won the 2025 Champions Trophy hosted in Pakistan/hybrid model.

The World Test Championship (WTC)

India have reached both WTC Finals (2021 and 2023) — the only team to do so — but lost both. In 2021, New Zealand beat India in seaming Southampton conditions that resembled NZ more than anything else. In 2023, Australia beat India under Pat Cummins at The Oval. India's WTC curse remains the one glaring gap in an otherwise glittering ICC record.

Chapter 06
India's Great Rivalries

India vs Pakistan — The World's Greatest Sporting Rivalry

No sporting contest on Earth commands the attention of more people simultaneously. When India play Pakistan in a World Cup, over a billion people watch. Political tensions, partition history, military conflicts — all of it pours into 22 yards of cricket. The two countries have not played bilateral series since 2013 due to political tensions, meaning every ICC event encounter feels like the final.

World Cup Head-to-Head

India lead Pakistan 7–0 in ODI World Cup matches — one of cricket's most remarkable records. Pakistan have never beaten India in an ODI World Cup match. The pressure on Pakistan in these games is immense.

Most Watched Games

India vs Pakistan at the 2023 World Cup in Ahmedabad drew 132,000 people to the Narendra Modi Stadium — the largest cricket crowd ever. Television audiences exceeded 300 million in India alone.

The premier modern Test rivalry. Named after Allan Border (Australia) and Sunil Gavaskar (India). India dominated this trophy from 2004 to 2024 — winning four consecutive series. The 2020–21 series in Australia — won by a depleted India — is considered one of the greatest series ever played.

India vs England — The Lord's & Home Rivalry

India's record at Lord's — the Home of Cricket — is poor historically. England's greatest bowlers — Anderson, Broad, Trueman, Laker — have tormented India on home soil. Yet India have had iconic moments: Ganguly's shirt-off celebration (2002), Laxman's 130 in the 2014 match, Ashwin's battles with Anderson. The 2021 series, led 2-1 by India before COVID intervened, showed India's new competitiveness in England.

India vs Sri Lanka — The Home-Away Split

At home in India, India dominate Sri Lanka. In Sri Lanka, conditions turn sharply and battles are intense. The 1996 WC semi-final — when a Kolkata crowd rioted after India's collapse and the game was awarded to Sri Lanka — remains the most painful India vs SL memory. Sri Lanka's 1996 WC win that year, built on Sanath Jayasuriya revolutionizing the powerplay, directly influenced India's own approach to limited-overs batting.

The IPL Revolution

The Indian Premier League (IPL) is not just a cricket tournament. It is the most influential sporting league launched in the 21st century — a complete reimagining of how cricket is played, watched, and sold. Every major T20 league in the world — Big Bash, PSL, SA20, CPL — exists because the IPL proved the model worked.

Origin — Lalit Modi's Vision

Commissioner Lalit Modi conceived the IPL in 2007 after India's shock exit from the 50-over World Cup. The model: franchise-based T20 cricket, city identity, player auctions, Bollywood glamour, big broadcast deals. The inaugural auction in January 2008 saw franchises sold for hundreds of millions of dollars. Players were bought for sums that made cricket — for the first time — genuinely rival football in earnings potential.

IPL's Impact on World Cricket

Player Economics

IPL created millionaire cricketers from associate nations. Afghan players (Rashid Khan), West Indians (Andre Russell), Sri Lankans — earning in 2 months what they'd earn in 5 years playing international cricket. The salary disparity is controversial — some players now prioritize IPL over national duty.

Skill Development

Young Indian players trained alongside Ponting, Warne, Gilchrist, Hayden — world-class coaches and players. The IPL compressed a generation of cricketing education into 2-month cycles. Players like Jasprit Bumrah, Hardik Pandya, and KL Rahul were shaped by IPL environments.

Global Reach

IPL broadcast deals reached every continent. JioCinema streamed 32M+ concurrent viewers for MI vs CSK in 2023 — a streaming record for any sport globally. IPL is now the second most watched domestic league in the world after the NFL.

The BCCI Effect

IPL revenue makes BCCI — Board of Control for Cricket in India — the wealthiest cricket board in the world by enormous margin. This financial dominance gives India extraordinary influence in the ICC, shaping global cricket scheduling, rules, and format decisions.

The Mega Auction

Every few years, the IPL holds a Mega Auction where all players are released and franchises rebuild from scratch. The 2022 mega auction featured players sold for ₹15 crore+ (roughly $2M). In 2024, Rishabh Pant was sold to Lucknow Super Giants for ₹27 crore ($3.2M) — the highest ever IPL auction price. The auction itself is a spectacle — broadcast live, analysed like a stock market, with experts debating franchise strategies.

Controversy

The IPL has not been without scandal. The 2013 spot-fixing scandal saw three players arrested and CSK & Rajasthan Royals banned for two years for involvement of team owners in betting. The BCCI's handling of the scandal — seen by many as protecting powerful interests — raised governance questions that persist.

"The IPL has done to cricket what the Premier League did to football. It has made it impossible to imagine cricket without it." — Harsha Bhogle, Cricket Commentator

Pitches & Conditions

Understanding pitch conditions is what separates a cricket enthusiast from a casual viewer. The pitch is not passive — it is an active, evolving participant in the game. An Indian pitch on Day 1 and Day 5 of a Test are almost different surfaces entirely.

The Indian Pitch — How it Works

India's pitches are made with black cotton soil (red soil in some regions) that cracks and turns as matches progress. On Day 1–2 of a Test, they can be flat and batting-friendly. By Day 4–5, the surface breaks up, producing uneven bounce and prodigious turn — the spinner's paradise. Visiting teams who struggle against spin are destroyed. Australia (2001, 2013), England (2012–13, 2016–17, 2021) and South Africa have all been humiliated by Indian pitches.

The SG Ball

India uses the SG (Sanspareils Greenlands) ball — manufactured in Meerut. Softer seam than the Kookaburra (used in Australia, NZ, SA) or Dukes (used in England). Swings early but deteriorates faster. Understanding which ball is in play is critical to understanding why conditions differ across countries.

Pitch Preparation Controversy

India has been accused of preparing pitches specifically designed to suit spin and neutralize visiting pace attacks — particularly against England in 2021 when pitches in Chennai and Ahmedabad were deemed too spin-friendly by ICC. India received a "poor" pitch rating. The BCCI pushed back fiercely.

Day-Night Test Cricket in India

India played their first Day-Night Test in 2019 at Eden Gardens against Bangladesh — a historic occasion with 65,000 fans under floodlights. The pink ball used in day-night Tests swings more than the red ball under lights and has a different seam, making it tricky for batsmen to pick up. India have dominated at home in day-night Tests.

Dew Factor

In many Indian venues, especially in winter months (October–February), evening dew settles on the outfield and the ball. A wet ball is extremely difficult to grip for spinners and becomes slippery for seamers too. Teams batting second benefit enormously — the damp outfield means shots travel faster to the boundary. This is why, in T20 matches at Indian venues, winning the toss and fielding first (to bowl in drier conditions, then bat in dew) is a significant strategic advantage.

Chapter 10
Cricket & India

In India, cricket is not a sport. It is identity, mythology, religion, politics, and emotion — all compressed into a single game. No other country on Earth relates to a sport the way India relates to cricket.

Cricket as Religion

When Tendulkar batted, offices emptied. When India lost a World Cup match, cities fell silent. Suicides have been reported after India's big losses. Temples have been built to Sachin Tendulkar in Tamil Nadu. MS Dhoni has been depicted as a deity. The parallels with religious devotion are not metaphorical — they are literal.

"Cricket is an Indian game accidentally discovered by the English. And we have remade it in our own image — loud, emotional, deeply personal." — Ramachandra Guha, Indian Historian

Cricket & Indian Independence

India's first cricket heroes — C.K. Nayudu, Lala Amarnath, Vinoo Mankad — played in the years around Independence. Beating England at cricket carried patriotic weight far beyond sport. The 1952 first Test win over England in Madras was celebrated like a second Independence. Each generation of Indian cricketers has carried the flag in its own way.

The BCCI — Most Powerful Board in World Cricket

The Board of Control for Cricket in India is the richest and most influential cricket board in the world. Its television deal with JioCinema and Star Sports generates revenues that dwarf every other board combined. The BCCI effectively controls the ICC — holding the largest single vote and providing the largest share of the ICC's global revenue. When India demands changes to scheduling, formats, or rules — the ICC listens. This financial power has reshaped cricket geopolitics entirely since the early 2000s.

Commentators Who Shaped India's Cricket Narrative

Harsha Bhogle

The voice of Indian cricket for a generation. Eloquent, passionate, balanced. His commentary on the 2011 WC final and the 2021 Gabba is the soundtrack of Indian cricket's greatest moments.

Ravi Shastri

"India-aa? India-aa!" His thunderous tone and iconic phrases defined 1980s–90s radio cricket. Later became India's head coach (2017–2021) through their SENA breakthrough.

Sunil Gavaskar

10,000 Test runs → equally sharp analyst. The most respected voice in Indian cricket criticism. Never afraid to speak hard truths about the team he helped build.

Cricket & Bollywood

India's two great obsessions — cricket and cinema — are inseparable. Bollywood has made films about cricket that were cultural events: Lagaan (nominated for the Academy Award), MS Dhoni: The Untold Story (blockbuster biopic), 83 (Kapil Dev's World Cup story). Shah Rukh Khan, the biggest Bollywood star, co-owns the Kolkata Knight Riders. Preity Zinta co-owns Punjab Kings. The two industries feed each other's audiences endlessly.

Gully Cricket — The Game's Soul

Before IPL, before T20, before broadcast deals — there was gully cricket. Played in alleys, streets, courtyards, beaches, and rooftops across India with taped balls, rubber balls, and improvised bats. Every great Indian cricketer played gully cricket. Tendulkar played at Shivaji Park, Mumbai. Dhoni in the dusty lanes of Ranchi. Kohli at Delhi's Vikaspuri. Gully cricket is where the love of the game is formed — and it is entirely India's own.

India's Cricketing Infrastructure

India has 30 first-class teams playing the Ranji Trophy — the world's largest domestic first-class competition. The Duleep Trophy (five zones) and Vijay Hazare Trophy (ODI format) feed talent to the IPL and the national team. India has over 20,000 cricket clubs and produces more professional cricketers per year than any other nation. The pyramid is unmatched in scale.

Chapter 11
Cricket Glossary

Master these terms — the language of Indian cricket conversation.

Ashwin
Not just a person — "doing an Ashwin" now means retiring from cricket mid-series or mid-tour, referencing R. Ashwin's retirement during the 2024 Australia series.
Batting first / Chasing
In T20s in India, due to dew, most teams prefer chasing (batting second). The team batting first must set a total high enough to overcome the dew-aided chase.
Bouncer
Short-pitched delivery rising sharply. Indian batsmen traditionally struggled with the short ball in SENA — Kohli's technical issues against outswing and short balls in England (2014) are the most discussed in Indian cricket history.
Carrom Ball
Delivery flicked by spinning the ball between the thumb and a bent middle finger — like a carrom board shot. Invented by R. Ashwin; also associated with Ajantha Mendis.
Corridor of Uncertainty
Ball delivered just outside off stump on a length — batsman unsure whether to play or leave. SENA bowlers exploit this corridor brilliantly against Indian batsmen.
Declaration
Captain voluntarily ends the innings before 10 wickets fall to set a target. Dhoni's use of declarations in Tests was often debated — he was seen as too conservative.
Doosra
Urdu for "the other one." An off-spinner's delivery that goes the opposite direction to the off-break. Harbhajan Singh's doosra was once considered illegal by the ICC.
DRS
Decision Review System. India refused to adopt DRS for years, with the BCCI citing technology trust issues. It is widely believed the real reason was that Dhoni — who rarely reviewed — would have been exposed statistically. India adopted it fully in 2016.
Duck
Dismissed for 0 runs. A "golden duck" is out first ball. Tendulkar was dismissed for 0 only 14 times in 200 Tests — extraordinarily rare for a 24-year career.
Follow-On
If trailing by 200+ runs in Tests, the fielding side can make opponents bat again immediately. India's 2001 Kolkata comeback — winning after being made to follow-on against Australia — is the most famous follow-on match in history.
Gully Cricket
Informal cricket played in streets, lanes, and courtyards across India. The breeding ground for every great Indian cricketer. Characterized by improvised rules, rubber balls (tennis ball cricket), and extraordinary creativity.
Helicopter Shot
MS Dhoni's patented shot — a full, low delivery hit over midwicket with the bat following through in a helicopter rotor motion. Generated enormous power. Originated from Dhoni's gully cricket days in Ranchi.
Mankad
Running out the non-striking batsman who backs up too far before the ball is delivered. Named after Vinoo Mankad (India) who dismissed Bill Brown (Australia) this way in 1947. Controversial — legal but considered unsportsmanlike until recently; now ICC-approved.
Nightwatchman
Lower-order batsman sent in near the end of day's play to protect specialist batsmen. India notably used Anil Kumble as nightwatchman, who then occasionally batted hours and scored valuable runs.
No-Ball / Free Hit
Illegal delivery. In T20s and ODIs, a no-ball results in a free hit — the next delivery from which the batsman cannot be dismissed except by a run-out. Changes match dynamics dramatically.
Over
6 consecutive legal deliveries. In IPL, a bowler can bowl a maximum of 4 overs. Death overs (overs 17–20) are often the most dramatic in T20 cricket.
Powerplay
First 6 overs in T20, first 10 in ODI — only 2 fielders allowed outside the 30-yard circle. Rohit Sharma's powerplay batting average is among the highest ever recorded.
Ranji Trophy
India's premier first-class domestic competition. Named after Ranjitsinhji — an Indian prince who played for England. Winning the Ranji Trophy is the most significant achievement for a domestic Indian cricketer.
Reverse Swing
Old ball swinging in the opposite direction to conventional swing. Waqar Younis and Wasim Akram mastered it against India in the 1990s. Zaheer Khan mastered it for India's benefit in the 2000s.
SENA
South Africa, England, New Zealand, Australia — the four nations where pitches favor pace and swing, making them historically the hardest destinations for subcontinental teams.
SG Ball
Sanspareils Greenlands ball — the Indian-made ball used in Tests on the subcontinent. Has a softer seam than the Kookaburra, swings less, and deteriorates faster.
Spin Quartet
Bedi, Prasanna, Chandrasekhar, Venkataraghavan — India's legendary four-spinner attack of the 1960s–70s. The most celebrated bowling unit in Indian cricket history.
Sticky Wicket
A wet pitch drying under sun — produces uneven bounce, extremely hard to bat on. Also a common English idiom meaning "a difficult situation."
Super Over
One-over eliminator used when T20/ODI matches are tied. Most famous Super Over: 2019 World Cup Final where both teams tied the Super Over and England won on boundary countback.
The Wall
Rahul Dravid's nickname — earned for his impenetrable defensive technique and ability to bat for hours. Scored 44,000+ balls faced in Tests — the most ever.
Ton / Century
Scoring 100 runs in a single innings. Tendulkar's 100 international centuries is the most revered batting record in cricket — a number likely never to be surpassed.
Toss
In Indian conditions — especially in Tests — the toss is crucial. Winning the toss and batting first on a fresh pitch before it turns is the most significant toss advantage in world cricket.
WTC
World Test Championship — ICC's 2-year Test league culminating in a final. India have reached both finals (2021, 2023) but lost both — the one glaring gap in India's ICC trophy collection.
Yorker
Full delivery aimed at the batsman's feet / base of stumps. Jasprit Bumrah's yorker — especially the in-swinging yorker to right-handed batsmen — is widely considered the most unplayable delivery in modern cricket.
